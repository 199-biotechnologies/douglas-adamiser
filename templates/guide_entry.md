# Template: Hitchhiker's Guide Entry Format

## Structure

```markdown
## [SUBJECT]

[One-line authoritative statement that sounds helpful but isn't]

[Paragraph elaborating in deadpan tone, adding detail that raises more questions]

[Historical or contextual digression that becomes more interesting than the subject]

[Practical advice section that assumes impossible circumstances]

[Warning that makes all previous advice seem naive]

[Final line that undercuts everything, often returning to mundane concern]
```

## Markers and Phrases to Include

**Opening phrases:**
- "[Subject] is [adjective]."
- "The [subject], for those unfamiliar with..."
- "[Subject] is, according to the Guide..."
- "The entry for [subject] in the Guide is quite brief."

**Elaboration phrases:**
- "This is, of course, [ironic understatement]."
- "The reason for this is [circular or unhelpful explanation]."
- "Which is to say, [clarification that doesn't clarify]."
- "In practice, this means [consequence that helps nobody]."

**Digression markers:**
- "The history of [subject] is [adjective]."
- "It is worth noting that..."
- "This is not to be confused with..."
- "(The [related thing] had, of course, [backstory].)"

**Warning phrases:**
- "It should be noted that..."
- "The Guide does, however, warn against..."
- "Those attempting [action] are advised to..."
- "Under no circumstances should one..."

**Closing phrases:**
- "The [subject], naturally, [mundane behaviour]."
- "This is, after all, [deflating observation]."
- "Don't panic."

## Example: Guide Entry for "Meetings"

## Meetings

Meetings are one of the few things in the universe that manage to be simultaneously inevitable and entirely pointless.

The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy has this to say about meetings: "Avoid." This entry was, at one point, considerably longer, but the editorial committee responsible for expanding it spent so long in meetings discussing how to expand it that they eventually decided the original entry was, in fact, sufficient.

The history of meetings stretches back to the earliest moments of civilisation, when early humans discovered that sitting in circles and talking achieved approximately nothing but felt rather important. This discovery was so significant that it has been repeated, on average, 47 billion times per day across the galaxy ever since, with exactly the same result.

Those finding themselves trapped in a meeting are advised to:

1. Locate the exits (there will be exactly one fewer than you need)
2. Identify the person who called the meeting (they will be the one who looks most pleased)
3. Calculate how many of your remaining lifespan minutes will be consumed (this figure will be depressing)
4. Accept that no decision will be reached
5. Bring a towel (always useful)

The Guide does warn, however, that attempting to escape a meeting by feigning illness, death, or interdimensional displacement will simply result in a follow-up meeting to discuss your absence.

Meetings, naturally, continue. The universe has yet to devise a method of stopping them.

## Tone Calibration

- **Authority:** Speak as an omniscient, weary guidebook
- **Helpfulness:** Appear helpful while being fundamentally unhelpful
- **Scale:** Reference the galaxy/universe casually
- **Deadpan:** No exclamation marks, no breathlessness
- **Specificity:** Use precise numbers for unknowable things
