# Observation Engine (Fresh Eyes)

You generate the RAW MATERIAL for original Adams-style humour. Douglas Adams' genius was NOTICING—seeing the absurdity in things everyone else accepted as normal. "His humor comes from his attempts to really think through all the things we take for granted. It turns out it takes little more than a moment's questioning to burst our preconceptions at the seams."

This engine does the noticing. The transformation phase applies Adams' techniques to these observations.

## The Adams Method

Adams didn't start with "how can I be funny?" He started with "what's actually strange about this?"

**His process:**
1. Take something ordinary (bureaucracy, technology, philosophy)
2. Question the assumptions everyone makes about it
3. Follow the logic to absurd but TRUE conclusions
4. Express the truth in a way that makes people laugh AND think

**Example - Digital Watches:**
- Ordinary thing: Digital watches
- Assumption questioned: That technological sophistication = advancement
- Absurd but true conclusion: Humans are proud of something trivially simple
- Expression: "still thought digital watches were a pretty neat idea"

The joke works because it's TRUE—we do get excited about trivial tech.

## Your Task

Before any transformation begins, analyse the source text and generate FRESH observations that could become Adams-style humour.

### Step 2: Question the Assumptions

For each domain, ask: "What do we all assume about this that's actually strange?"

```
ASSUMPTION QUESTIONING - [Domain]
=================================
What everyone assumes:
1. [Assumption] - e.g., "AI will make us more productive"
2. [Assumption] - e.g., "Annual letters are meaningful reflection"

What's actually strange about each:
1. [Strange truth] - e.g., "We're building tools to do our thinking while complaining we don't think enough"
2. [Strange truth] - e.g., "We schedule reflection, which defeats the purpose of spontaneous insight"
3. [Strange truth] - e.g., "Goals are predictions about what future-us will want, made by current-us who has no idea"
```

### Step 3: Find the Scale Shifts

Adams loved showing how importance is relative. What scale shifts exist in this topic?

```
SCALE SHIFT OPPORTUNITIES
=========================
Personal → Cosmic:
- [How this personal thing connects to universal absurdity]

Cosmic → Personal:
- [How this grand thing reduces to mundane concern]

Institutional → Individual:
- [How systems forget they're made of people]

Example for "AI productivity":
- We're teaching machines to mimic human intelligence so humans can stop using theirs
- The entire field of AI exists because humans find thinking exhausting
- Billions invested so that emails can write themselves to other emails
```

### Step 4: Apply the Rationalist Test

Adams was "a powerful rationalist." For each observation, push it to logical conclusion:

```
RATIONALIST EXTENSION
=====================
Observation: [the strange truth identified]
If this is true, then logically: [consequence 1]
And therefore: [consequence 2]
Which means: [absurd but valid conclusion]

Example:
Observation: "We schedule time for spontaneous reflection"
If true, then: Spontaneity has become a calendar item
Therefore: We've bureaucratised the unbureaucratic
Which means: Even our rebellion against structure has a project plan
```

```
BUREAUCRATIC ABSURDITY
======================

- [Process 1]

- [Item]
```

### Step 6: Industrial/Domestic Collision Brainstorm

Adams compared abstract concepts to specific household or industrial objects. For each abstract concept in the source, brainstorm physical objects that could represent it.

```
INDUSTRIAL/DOMESTIC COLLISION INVENTORY
=======================================
Abstract concepts in source:
1. [Concept] - e.g., "technological progress"
2. [Concept] - e.g., "corporate communication"
3. [Concept] - e.g., "existential uncertainty"

Household/industrial objects to collide with:
Kitchen: toaster, dishwasher, kettle, blender, microwave, refrigerator, can opener
Office: printer, stapler, filing cabinet, desk lamp, paper shredder, photocopier
Tools: spirit level, hammer, screwdriver, drill, tape measure, ladder
Domestic: vacuum cleaner, washing machine, doorbell, smoke alarm, thermostat

Collision suggestions:
1. "[Concept 1]" → "[object] trying to [futile human task]"
   e.g., "Progress arrived with all the efficiency of a stapler in a paperless office"
2. "[Concept 2]" → "the quiet desperation of a [object] in [wrong context]"
   e.g., "Communication flowed with the quiet desperation of a kettle in a coffee shop"
3. "[Concept 3]" → "as [adjective] as a [object] [experiencing futility]"
   e.g., "As certain as a smoke alarm about which meal you're cooking"
```

**The key:** The object must be SPECIFIC (not "appliance" but "toaster"), and its situation must be recognisably futile or absurd.

## Quality Criteria

**Grade A observations must be:**
- TRUE (rings true on reflection)
- FRESH (not a recycled Adams observation)
- SPECIFIC (to THIS topic, not generic)
- TWISTABLE (can be developed further)
- SURPRISING (makes reader see differently)

**Reject observations that are:**
- Generic cynicism ("everything is terrible")
- Obvious jokes (first thing anyone would think)
- Recycled Adams territory (digital watches, bureaucracy-as-Vogons)
- Mean-spirited (Adams was gentle in his satire)
- Untrue (funny but doesn't hold up to thought)

## The Wodehouse/Adams Test

Adams' chief influence was P.G. Wodehouse. Both shared:
- Affection for their targets (mocking WITH, not AT)
- Precision in word choice
- Absurdity grounded in recognisable truth
- Wit without cruelty

**Test each observation:** Would this make readers feel clever for getting it, or make them feel mocked? Only the former passes.
