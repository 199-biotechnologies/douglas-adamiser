# Adams Vocabulary Guide

## The Adams Lexical Signature

Adams has distinctive word patterns. This guide provides vocabulary markers, phrase templates, and neologism systems to achieve authentic Adams voice.

---

## Part 1: Characteristic Intensifiers

Adams uses specific intensifiers that signal his voice:

### Favoured Intensifiers
- **utterly** (not "completely")
- **perfectly** (for imperfect things)
- **entirely** (especially when nothing is entire)
- **thoroughly** (for half-done things)
- **marginally** (understatement)
- **slightly** (cosmic understatement)
- **rather** (British understatement supreme)
- **somewhat** (to make disasters mild)
- **practically** (to make impractical seem possible)
- **theoretically** (to signal something won't work)
- **technically** (to signal loopholes)

### Intensifiers to Avoid
- very (too weak)
- really (too casual)
- super (too American)
- incredibly (overstates)
- amazingly (too breathless)
- literally (overused, save for comic effect)

## Part 2: Characteristic Adjective Patterns

### Pre-noun Adjective Stacks

Adams stacks adjectives in specific patterns:

**Pattern A: Scale + Quality + Specificity**
> "a small, perfectly rectangular, somewhat depressed robot"

**Pattern B: Contradictory Pair + Noun**
> "the warm, cold expanse of space"

**Pattern C: Technical + Emotional + Noun**
> "the thermodynamically improbable, vaguely resentful cup of tea"

### Adjectives Adams Loves
- improbable
- infinite
- preposterously
- marginally
- unremarkable
- unfashionable
- uncharted
- unregarded
- insignificant
- bewildered
- thoroughly
- mostly
- almost

## Part 3: Signature Phrase Patterns

### The Deadpan Definition
```
"[Subject] was [adjective]. Not [expected alternative], exactly, but [unexpected qualifier] [adjective]."
```
> "The ship was fast. Not fast in the conventional sense, or indeed any sense that would be useful, but existentially fast."

### The Reluctant Clarification
```
"Or rather, [correction]. Or, more accurately, [further correction]. Or, to be perfectly precise, [admission of complete uncertainty]."
```

### The Qualified Absolute
```
"It was, almost certainly, [absolute statement], give or take [absurd qualifier]."
```
> "It was, almost certainly, the worst poetry in the universe, give or take a few teenage diaries."

### The Inverted Comparison
```
"[Subject] [verb] in much the same way that [opposite thing] [doesn't/can't]."
```

### The Helpful Unhelpfulness
```
"The [term], for those unfamiliar, refers to [definition]. This means, in practice, [consequence that helps no one]."
```

## Part 4: British English Markers

Adams is emphatically British. Key markers:

### Spelling
- colour, honour, favour (not -or)
- centre, theatre, metre (not -er)
- realise, organise (not -ize, though both are technically British)
- travelling, cancelled (double l)
- programme (not program, unless computing)
- grey (not gray)

### Vocabulary
- flat (not apartment)
- lift (not elevator)
- queue (not line)
- whilst (not while, though both acceptable)
- autumn (not fall)
- holiday (not vacation)
- post (not mail)
- biscuit (not cookie)
- rubbish (not garbage/trash)

### Phrases
- "rather" as intensifier
- "quite" meaning "somewhat" not "very"
- "I should think" (not "I would think")
- "couldn't be bothered" (not "didn't feel like it")
- "turned out" (not "ended up")

## Part 5: The Adams Neologism System

Adams invented words that feel like they should exist. His system:

### Type A: The Linguistic Gap Fill
Words for things that exist but lack names (see: *The Meaning of Liff*).

**Formula:** Take place name, assign to common but unnamed experience.
- Shoeburyness: the feeling of sitting on a warm seat
- Woking: forgetting why you entered a room

### Type B: The Technical Absurdity
Scientific-sounding words for impossible things.

**Formula:** Latin/Greek root + Scientific suffix + Impossible modifier
- Bistromathics: mathematical principles that work differently in restaurants
- Hooloovoo: a super-intelligent shade of the colour blue
- Peril Sensitive Sunglasses: darken at the first sign of danger

### Type C: The Compound Impossibility
Combine existing words into new absurdities.

**Formula:** [Adjective] + [Noun] that creates conceptual paradox
- Infinite Improbability Drive
- Total Perspective Vortex
- Point-of-View Gun

## Part 7: Forbidden Vocabulary

Words Adams would never use unironically:

- awesome (American)
- basically (filler)
- actually (overused)
- literally (unless for comic effect)
- super (as intensifier)
- totally (too Californian)
- guys (too casual)
- stuff (too vague—unless deliberately so)
- cool (dated American)
- massive (British but not Adams)

## Part 9: The Read-Aloud Voice Test

Adams' vocabulary creates a specific spoken rhythm:

**Test:** Read passage aloud in a slightly weary, educated British voice.

**Check for:**
- Words that feel wrong in that voice
- Phrases that feel too American
- Intensifiers that feel breathless
- Technical terms that stay technical

**If it sounds like Stephen Fry would enjoy reading it aloud, it's Adams.**

If it sounds like an enthusiastic American tech blogger, revise.
